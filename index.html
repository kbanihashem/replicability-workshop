Overview

Scientific progress in machine learning and statistics fundamentally relies on reproducibility (the ability to verify results with the same data and code) and replicability (the ability to obtain consistent conclusions on new data). As modern AI systems grow in scale and complexity—with non-convex optimization, stochastic training, and sensitivity to initialization—ensuring replicability has become a core methodological challenge.

For the AI and ML community, this issue is central to maintaining statistical validity and algorithmic reliability. Failures in reproducibility erode trust in published research and obstruct the translation of theoretical advances into dependable real-world systems.

Recent theoretical progress has begun to formalize replicable algorithm design—a concept introduced by Impagliazzo, Lei, Pitassi, and Sorrell (STOC 2022)—as a mathematical foundation for reproducibility. This notion provides a rigorous framework for understanding when an algorithm consistently produces the same output under independent sampling, even in randomized or high-dimensional settings. Follow-up studies have linked replicability to privacy, adaptive generalization, and algorithmic stability, revealing deep structural connections and open challenges in designing provably replicable algorithms for learning and optimization.

This workshop aims to bridge theoretical advances and practical reproducibility tools, uniting algorithm designers, statistical theorists, and practitioners. Key questions include:

  • What formal frameworks best capture reproducibility in randomized and adaptive algorithms?
  • How can theory guide replicable system and experimental pipeline design?
  • What are the limits of provable replicability under computational, statistical, or privacy constraints?

By bringing together diverse perspectives from theory, methodology, and practice, this workshop will lay conceptual foundations for the next generation of reliable and verifiable AI research.
